Knife Skills: The Essential Cuts Every Cook Should Know

Consistent knife cuts aren't about looks — they're about even cooking. Here's the professional system for cutting vegetables correctly.

By Chef Gabe 2026-03-25 7 min read

In a professional kitchen, knife skills are the first thing apprentices learn and the last thing they fully master. The reason is simple: consistent cuts produce consistent cooking. Vegetables cut to different sizes cook at different rates — the small pieces overcook while the large ones remain underdone. Uniform cuts mean everything finishes at the same time.

Your Most Important Investment: A Sharp Knife

Before any technique discussion, this must be said: a dull knife is more dangerous than a sharp one. A dull knife requires more force, which leads to slipping and losing control. A sharp knife cuts with minimal pressure and stays where you direct it.

Buy one good 8-inch chef's knife. Keep it sharp with a honing steel (which realigns the edge) before every use. Have it professionally sharpened or use a whetstone 2–3 times per year. This single habit will transform your cooking more than any other piece of equipment.

The Grip: How Professionals Hold a Knife

The correct grip is the "pinch grip." Place your index finger and thumb on either side of the blade, right at the bolster (the thick part where blade meets handle). Wrap your other three fingers around the handle. This gives maximum control and reduces fatigue over long prep sessions.

Your non-knife hand forms the "claw grip" on the food — fingers curled under, knuckles forward, guiding the knife. The flat side of the blade rests against your knuckles as you cut, which prevents the blade from hitting your fingertips. This is non-negotiable for safe, fast cutting.

The Essential Cuts

The Slice

The most basic cut — a straight cut through food at varying thicknesses. Use a rocking motion: the tip of the knife stays in contact with the board while the heel rocks up and down. Don't lift the whole blade for each cut.

The Julienne (Matchstick)

Thin strips about 3mm × 3mm × 6cm (1/8" × 1/8" × 2.5"). Used for: stir-fry, garnishes, salads. Method: slice the vegetable into planks, stack the planks, and cut into thin strips.

The Brunoise (Fine Dice)

Tiny cubes of about 3mm. Used for: soffritto, sauces, garnishes. Method: julienne first, then cut the julienned strips crosswise into tiny cubes.

The Small Dice

Cubes of about 6mm (¼"). Used for: soffritto, soups, stews. Method: cut planks, stack, cut into strips, cut crosswise into cubes.

The Medium Dice

Cubes of about 12mm (½"). The most common dice for home cooking. Used in virtually everything from stews to salads.

The Chiffonade (Herb Ribbons)

Used for leafy herbs (basil, mint, sage) and leafy greens. Stack leaves, roll into a tight cylinder, slice crosswise into thin ribbons. The rolling prevents bruising and tearing.

The Mince

Very fine chopping, used for garlic and shallots. Rock the knife over the roughly chopped pieces repeatedly, pivoting on the tip, until pieces are as fine as possible.

The mise en place mindset: In professional kitchens, all vegetables are prepped before cooking begins. This French concept — "everything in its place" — means you cut everything, organize it by cooking order, and then cook without stopping. It's the reason restaurant cooking looks effortless. Adopt it at home.

Caring for Your Cutting Board

Use wood or plastic boards — never glass or ceramic, which destroy knife edges instantly. Keep a separate board for raw meat. Oil wooden boards monthly with food-grade mineral oil to prevent cracking. Replace plastic boards when they develop deep grooves that harbor bacteria.
